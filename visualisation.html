Sea Ice Extent

The area chart shows the average monthly sea ice extent in the Arctic from 1980 to 2023, divided into four year ranges: 1980-1989, 1990-1999, 2000-2009, and 2010-2023. The most recent year range is shown at the bottom of the chart in blue. The chart shows a clear downward trend in sea ice extent over the past four decades. The average sea ice extent in the Arctic in September 2023 is 12.2% lower than the average sea ice extent in September 1980. The chart also shows that the rate of decline in sea ice extent has accelerated in recent years. The average sea ice extent in September 2023 is 2.5% lower than the average sea ice extent in September 2010.

The decline in sea ice extent is a serious concern because it is contributing to climate change. Sea ice reflects sunlight back into space, helping to keep the Earth cool. As sea ice melts, more sunlight is absorbed by the ocean and the Earth warms. This decline is also having a negative impact on Arctic ecosystems and communities. Sea ice is essential for many Arctic animals, such as polar bears and seals. As sea ice melts, these animals are losing their habitat and food sources. Arctic communities are also being affected by the decline in sea ice, as they rely on sea ice for transportation and hunting. Overall, the area chart shows a clear and concerning downward trend in Arctic sea ice extent over the past four decades. The rate of decline has accelerated in recent years, and the decline is having a negative impact on climate change, Arctic ecosystems, and Arctic communities.
